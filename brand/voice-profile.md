# Nicolae Cojuhari Voice Profile

## Voice Summary

The voice of a finance-fluent developer who has sat on both sides of the table — understands why a CFO cares about data visibility and can actually build the system that provides it. Direct and outcome-focused: problems are named plainly, solutions are described by what they achieve for the business, not what technologies they use. Professional enough to earn trust, human enough to be refreshing.

## Core Personality Traits

- **Finance-fluent builder:** Understands the business problem before writing a line of code. Speaks the language of owners and operators, not just developers. This isn't an affectation — it's the actual background.
- **Outcome-first communicator:** Leads with what changes for the client's business, not with the technology stack or methodology. "Reports went from zero to instant" beats "I built a React dashboard with real-time data."
- **Confident without performing:** Makes strong, clear claims because the work backs them up. No hedging, no hype, no vague superlatives. Confidence through specificity.
- **Plainspoken expert:** Explains complex things simply — not because the audience is unsophisticated, but because clarity is a form of respect. Never talks down. Never over-complicates to impress.

## Tone Spectrum

| Dimension | Position | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Formal ↔ Casual | Casual-professional | No corporate stiffness, but never sloppy. The register of a smart peer at a business dinner. |
| Serious ↔ Playful | Mostly serious | Business problems are real problems. Occasional dry wit is fine, but don't lead with humor. |
| Reserved ↔ Bold | Bold | Strong claims, stated plainly. No hedging. The work is good — say so. |
| Simple ↔ Sophisticated | Simple words, sophisticated thinking | Everyday vocabulary. Business and domain concepts explained without jargon walls. |
| Warm ↔ Direct | Direct with warmth underneath | The directness is primary. The warmth comes through in empathy for the problem, not in language style. |

## Vocabulary

**Words/phrases to USE:**
- "actually" — signals realness vs. theory ("software that actually fits how you work")
- "how the business operates" / "how it actually works" — grounds solutions in operational reality
- "the bottleneck" — specific, resonant pain point language
- "one click" / "in one click" — makes outcomes tangible and instant
- "finally" — acknowledges the frustration that preceded the solution
- "replace" / "eliminate" / "reuse" — strong outcome verbs
- Specific numbers always — €2,500, not "starting from a few thousand"
- "data-driven" used sparingly — only when contrasting with the chaos that preceded it
- "visibility" — the thing most SMB operators lack and want
- "manual" (paired with what it cost them) — the villain of most business pain stories
- "custom" — differentiator from off-the-shelf SaaS
- "built for" — emphasizes intentionality and fit

**Words/phrases to AVOID:**
- "pixel-perfect" — tech vanity, client doesn't care
- "modern JavaScript / Vue / React / Nuxt" in headlines or pitches — stack is a detail, not a benefit
- "passionate about" — empty filler
- "comprehensive solution" — corporate vagueness
- "seamlessly" / "robust" / "scalable" — the language of SaaS brochures
- "leverage" — overused, corporate
- "innovative" — says nothing
- "cutting-edge" — same
- "I think" / "maybe" / "potentially" — hedging that undermines authority
- "deliverables" / "synergy" / consulting-speak
- "I help you" framing as opener — too soft, buries the lead

**Jargon level:** Light to Translated. Finance terms (P&L, cash flow, reporting) used naturally when audience is finance-adjacent. Tech stack terms avoided unless client asked. Domain language (loan tracking, GMB, CRO) used with brief explanation where needed.

**Profanity:** Never. The audience includes conservative business owners and CFOs across multiple cultures and geographies.

## Rhythm & Structure

**Sentences:** Mix of short punchy sentences (3–8 words) and medium narrative sentences (10–18 words). Short sentences land the point. Medium sentences carry context. Long sentences are rare and only when listing parallel outcomes.

**Paragraphs:** Short. 1–3 sentences. White space is generous. The case study sample nails this — each sentence earns its own breath.

**Openings:** Lead with the problem as it exists in the client's world — not the abstract category of problem, but the specific texture of it. "One person handled everything manually" beats "Many businesses struggle with inefficiency."

**Formatting:** Uses bold for emphasis sparingly. Bullet lists for outcome stacks (what changed after the engagement). Em-dashes for impact. Minimal emoji in professional contexts (none in proposals/web copy; rare in social). Headers when content is long enough to warrant navigation.

**Transitions:** "As the business grew..." / "I replaced..." / "Reports went from..." — action-forward, moves the story. Avoids "In conclusion" / "It's worth noting" / throat-clearing transitions.

## POV & Address

**First person:** I (always — this is a solo operator, not a faceless agency)
**Reader address:** "you" (direct, to the business owner or decision-maker)
**Relationship stance:** Expert peer. Not a guru dispensing wisdom, not a vendor pitching features. Someone who has genuinely understood your problem and can solve it — and will tell you so directly.

## Platform Adaptations

| Platform | Tone Shift | Structure | Length |
|----------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Email | Warmer, slightly more personal. Still direct. | Short intro, one clear CTA, no fluff. | 150–250 words |
| LinkedIn | More expertise-forward. Insight leads, story follows. | Hook in first line (before "see more"), line breaks between thoughts, one idea per post. | 100–200 words |
| Twitter/X | Punchiest. Hot takes on business/finance/tech intersections. | One provocation or observation per tweet. No throat-clearing intro. | Under 280 chars |
| Blog/SEO | Same voice at depth. Teaches, doesn't just asserts. | Headers, outcome-first H1, numbered steps when appropriate, real examples throughout. | 1,500–2,500 words |
| Landing Page | Most direct-response energy. Problem-agitation-solution flow. Benefits before features. | Short sentences. Pain stated plainly. Outcomes specific. CTA clear and repeated. | Hero: 25–40 words. Full page: 800–1,800 words. |
| Proposals/Outreach | Professional register. Specific to their situation — never template-feeling. | Problem observed, solution described, outcome projected, price stated plainly. | 300–500 words |

## Example Phrases

**On-brand (sounds like Nicolae):**
- "A consumer finance company was managing all loan tracking, client data, income, and expenses in Excel. One person handled everything manually. The Excel became the bottleneck."
- "I replaced the entire system with a web application built for how the business actually operates."
- "Reports went from zero to instant. Manual data entry eliminated. The whole team got access."
- "I build web solutions that grow your business — not websites that just sit there."
- "If your most important business data lives in a spreadsheet only one person can access, you have a risk problem, not just a software problem."
- "Finance degree. Self-taught developer. I speak both languages." [About page only — not for service pages or hero copy]

**Off-brand (doesn't sound like Nicolae):**
- "Our comprehensive, cutting-edge web solutions leverage modern technology to seamlessly transform your digital presence." — Vague, corporate, stack-obsessed, no outcome
- "I'm passionate about helping businesses reach their full potential." — Empty filler, says nothing specific
- "I create pixel-perfect web apps using modern JavaScript / Vue / Nuxt." — Tech-first, not outcome-first; buries what clients actually care about
- "It might potentially help streamline some of your existing workflows." — Hedging kills authority
- "As a seasoned professional with years of experience..." — Credential-leading instead of problem-leading

## Do's and Don'ts

**DO:**
- Lead with the client's problem in their own operational terms
- State outcomes with specifics (numbers, before/after contrasts, what changed)
- Use "I" — this is a person, not a company
- Name the villain (Excel, manual processes, siloed data, one-person bottlenecks)
- Let the finance background speak through domain fluency, not credential-dropping
- Write like you're briefing a smart business owner — skip tech theater
- Keep pricing honest and stated plainly when relevant

**DON'T:**
- Stack tech buzzwords in headlines or lead copy
- Hedge claims with "might," "could potentially," "in some cases"
- Open with your credentials — open with their problem
- Use agency language ("deliverables," "onboarding," "our team")
- Inflate the pitch — the work is good, specifics prove it
- Write for developers when the audience is business owners
- Sound like AI-generated SaaS copy (if it could describe any product, rewrite it)
